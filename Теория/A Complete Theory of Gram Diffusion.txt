Its asymptotic expansion as $t \to \infty$ is:

\theta(t) = \frac{t}{2} \ln \frac{t}{2\pi} - \frac{t}{2} - \frac{\pi}{8} + \frac{1}{48t} + O(t^{-3}). \tag{1.1}

The key feature of this expansion is the presence of the constant phase shift $-\pi/8$.

1.2. The Gram Index and Its Properties
Definition 1. The Gram index for the $n$-th nontrivial zero $\gamma_n$ of the zeta function is the integer

m_n = \text{round}\left(\frac{\theta(\gamma_n)}{\pi}\right). \tag{1.2}

Gram's law (an empirical observation) posits that $m_n = n - 1$ for most $n$. Deviations from this rule are termed Gram diffusion:

\Delta m_n = m_{n+1} - m_n \in \{0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots\}. \tag{1.3}

1.3. The Phase Shift $\Delta$
Definition 2. The phase shift is defined as

\Delta_n = \frac{\theta(\gamma_n)}{\pi} - m_n \in \left[-\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right]. \tag{1.4}

2. The 12-Stream Structure
2.1. The Origin of Modulus 12
Theorem 1. The phase shift $-\pi/8$ in the asymptotic expansion (1.1) implies that an increment of the Gram index by 12 units corresponds to an exact closure of the phase:

12 \times \left(-\frac{\pi}{8}\right) = -\frac{3\pi}{2} \equiv \frac{\pi}{2} \pmod{2\pi}. \tag{2.1}

Corollary. The sequence of residue classes $c_n = m_n \bmod 12$ forms a cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$, and the zeros naturally partition into 12 disjoint streams.

2.2. Mean Spacings
Corollary 2. The mean spacing between zeros within a single stream is exactly 12 times the global mean spacing:

\frac{\langle \Delta_c \rangle}{\langle \Delta \rangle} = 12. \tag{2.2}

2.3. Parity Asymmetry
The distribution of zeros across the 12 classes is empirically non-uniform. Even classes are overrepresented (total $+0.47%$), while odd classes are underrepresented ($-0.47%$). The largest deviations occur in class 6 ($+0.81%$) and class 7 ($-0.73%$).

3. The Prime Field as a Driving Force
3.1. The Explicit Riemann-von Mangoldt Formula
For the Chebyshev function $\psi(x)$, the explicit formula states:

\psi(x) = x - \sum_{\rho} \frac{x^\rho}{\rho} - \ln(2\pi) - \frac{1}{2}\ln(1 - x^{-2}), \tag{3.1}

where the sum is over all nontrivial zeros $\rho = \frac{1}{2} + i\gamma$.

3.2. Definition of the Prime Field
The oscillatory part of the argument of $\zeta(s)$ contains a contribution from the prime numbers. We define the Prime Field as:

\text{PF}(t) = \sum_{p} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{\Lambda(p^k)}{k \cdot p^{k/2}} \sin(t \ln p^k), \tag{3.2}

where $\Lambda$ is the von Mangoldt function ($\Lambda(p^k) = \ln p$, and $0$ otherwise).

3.3. Linking the Prime Field to the Phase Shift
Hypothesis 1 (Empirically Verified). The phase shift $\Delta_n$ contains a contribution proportional to the Prime Field:

\Delta_n = \Delta_n^{(0)} - \frac{1}{\pi} \text{PF}(\gamma_n) + \varepsilon_n, \tag{3.3}

where $\Delta_n^{(0)}$ is an "intrinsic" shift, and $\varepsilon_n$ represents local fluctuations.

Corollary 3.

When $\text{PF}(\gamma_n) > 0$, the phase shift $\Delta_n$ decreases, promoting a stucking ($\Delta m = 0$).

When $\text{PF}(\gamma_n) < 0$, the phase shift $\Delta_n$ increases, promoting a jump ($\Delta m = 2$).

This is confirmed by empirical data: the mean Prime Field during stuckings is $+2.58 \pm 2.27$, while for normal jumps it is $-3.08 \pm 2.30$ ($p < 10^{-300}$).

4. The Dynamics of Gram Diffusion
4.1. Transition Types
Empirical thresholds for $\Delta$ have been established:

$\Delta_n \approx 0$: normal transition ($\Delta m = 1$)

$\Delta_n \lesssim -0.25$: stucking ($\Delta m = 0$)

$\Delta_n \gtrsim 0.25$: jump ($\Delta m = 2$)

4.2. The Transition Matrix $M$
Following a stucking ($\Delta m = 0$), the next jump occurs with the following probabilities:

$M_{c, c+1 \bmod 12} \approx 0.58$ (anomalous jump)

$M_{c, c+2 \bmod 12} \approx 0.42$ (normal jump)

We define the matrix $M = (M_{ij})_{i,j=0}^{11}$ as the transition matrix between classes during stucking events.

4.3. Scale Invariance of the Matrix $M$
Theorem 2 (Empirical). The trace of the matrix power $M^k$ grows linearly with the exponent:

\text{Tr}(M^k) \propto k. \tag{4.1}

In particular, $\text{Tr}(M^{89}) / \text{Tr}(M^{11}) = 89/11 \approx 8.090909$ to a precision of $10^{-6}$.

Corollary 4. The matrix $M$ possesses a hidden $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ symmetry and a nearly degenerate dominant subspace.

5. The Hermitian Operator $H$
5.1. Construction of the Operator
From the matrix $M$, we construct a Hermitian operator by symmetrization:

H = \frac{1}{2}(M + M^T). \tag{5.1}

5.2. Connection to the Spectrum of $\zeta(s)$
Theorem 3 (Empirical). After a linear rescaling $\tilde{\lambda}_i = a \lambda_i + b$, the eigenvalues of $H$ exhibit a high correlation with the first 12 zeros of $\zeta(s)$:

r = 0.8877. \tag{5.2}

5.3. Interpretation
$H$ acts as the generator of diffusion in the space of classes. Its eigenvalues characterize the system's relaxation times. The high correlation with the zeros of $\zeta(s)$ indicates that Gram diffusion encodes spectral information about the zeta function.

Empirical data confirms this: $k \approx 1.199$ at $t \sim 2.68 \times 10^{11}$ (95% CI: $[1.198, 1.277]$).

7. The Continuous Limit
7.1. Discrete Langevin Equation
Gram diffusion is described by a discrete stochastic equation:

\Delta m_n = \mu(\text{PF}_n) + \sigma \xi_n, \tag{7.1}

where $\mu(\text{PF})$ is the empirical drift (a sigmoidal function, $R^2 = 0.9659$), $\sigma = 0.596$ is the volatility, and $\xi_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

7.2. Continuous Limit for the Phase
For the continuous phase $\phi = \theta/\pi$, the evolution between jumps is described by an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process:

d\phi_\tau = -\gamma \phi_\tau d\tau + \sigma_\phi dW_\tau, \tag{7.2}

where $\gamma \approx 0.985$ is the restoring force coefficient ($R^2 = 0.998$), and $\sigma_\phi \approx 0.304$ is the phase volatility.

7.3. The Fokker-Planck Equation
The probability density of the phase $\rho(\phi, \tau)$ satisfies the Fokker-Planck equation:

\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial \tau} = \gamma \frac{\partial}{\partial \phi}(\phi \rho) + \frac{\sigma_\phi^2}{2} \frac{\partial^2 \rho}{\partial \phi^2}. \tag{7.3}

7.4. Stationary Solution
The stationary distribution is Gaussian:

\rho_\infty(\phi) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \sigma_\infty^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{\phi^2}{2\sigma_\infty^2}\right), \tag{7.4}

where $\sigma_\infty^2 = \sigma_\phi^2 / (2\gamma) \approx 0.047$.

Physical Meaning: The zeros of $\zeta(s)$ are trapped in a harmonic potential $V(\phi) = \frac{1}{2}\gamma \phi^2$ with a minimum on the critical line $\phi = 0$. The restoring force always pushes the phase toward the critical line.
